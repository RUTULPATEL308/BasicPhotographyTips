Master Camera Exposure

Understanding exposure is fundamental to photography. Learn how aperture, shutter speed, and ISO work together to create perfectly exposed images in any lighting condition.

Exposure Compensation

Even in automatic modes, you can override your camera's exposure decisions using exposure compensation. This allows you to make images brighter or darker while maintaining the convenience of semi-automatic modes.

When to Use Exposure Compensation:

  • Snow/Beach: +1 to +2 EV (camera underexposes bright scenes)
  • Backlit Subjects: +0.5 to +1 EV (brighten shadowed faces)
  • Dark Subjects: -0.5 to -1 EV (prevent overexposure)
  • Creative Effect: Adjust to match your artistic vision

Camera Metering Modes

How your camera measures light to determine exposure

Matrix/Evaluative

Analyzes the entire frame using multiple zones. Best general-purpose metering mode.

Use for: Most situations, landscapes, general photography

Center-Weighted

Emphasizes the center of the frame while considering the entire scene.

Use for: Portraits, subjects in center of frame

Spot Metering

Measures light from a very small area (1-5% of frame). Most precise control.

Use for: Backlit subjects, high contrast scenes, precise control

Exposure Tips & Techniques

01

Expose to the Right

Slightly overexpose (without clipping highlights) to capture maximum image data and reduce noise.

02

Watch Your Histogram

Use the histogram to check exposure. Avoid clipping highlights (right edge) and shadows (left edge).

03

Bracket Important Shots

Take multiple exposures of critical shots to ensure you capture the perfect exposure.

04

Learn Your Camera's Limits

Test your camera's high ISO performance to know when image quality becomes unacceptable.

05

Use a Tripod for Slow Shutter

Speeds slower than 1/60s usually require a tripod to avoid camera shake blur.

06

Shoot in RAW

RAW files give you much more flexibility to correct exposure mistakes in post-processing.
